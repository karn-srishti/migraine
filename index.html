Migraine

What is Migraine?

Migraine is a neurological condition characterized by recurring headaches that can be moderate to severe. It is often accompanied by other symptoms like sensitivity to light and sound, nausea, and vomiting. Migraine headaches can last for hours or even days, and they can significantly interfere with a person's daily activities.

Causes of Migraine

The exact cause of migraine is not fully understood, but there are several factors that are believed to contribute to the development of this condition:

  • Genetics: Migraine tends to run in families, which suggests a genetic component.
  • Environmental factors: Changes in weather, altitude, or air pressure can trigger migraines in some people.
  • Hormonal changes: Women are more likely to experience migraines than men, and many women report that their migraines are associated with their menstrual cycle.
  • Food triggers: Certain foods and beverages like aged cheese, alcohol, and chocolate have been known to trigger migraines in some people.
  • Stress: Emotional stress, physical stress, or changes in routine can trigger migraines in some people.

Symptoms of Migraine

The symptoms of migraine can vary from person to person, but the most common symptoms include:

  • Headache pain that is typically on one side of the head
  • Sensitivity to light, sound, or smell
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Visual disturbances like flashes of light or blind spots
  • Tingling or numbness in the face or hands

Treatment for Migraine

There is no cure for migraine, but there are several treatment options that can help manage the symptoms and reduce the frequency of migraine attacks:

  • Medications: Over-the-counter pain relievers like ibuprofen or aspirin can be effective for mild to moderate migraines. Prescription medications like triptans or ergotamines can be used for more severe migraines.
  • Lifestyle changes: Getting enough sleep, eating a healthy diet, staying hydrated, and avoiding triggers like stress and certain foods can help reduce the frequency of migraines.
  • Alternative therapies: Acupuncture, massage, and cognitive behavioral therapy have all been shown to be effective for some people with migraines.

Prevention of Migraine

While there is no surefire way to prevent migraines, there are several strategies that can help reduce the frequency of attacks:

  • Identifying triggers: Keeping a headache diary can help you identify triggers like certain foods, weather changes, or stressors that may be contributing to your migraines.
  • Lifestyle changes: Getting enough sleep, eating a healthy diet, staying hydrated, and avoiding triggers like stress and certain foods can help reduce the frequency of migraines.
  • Medications: Prescription medications like beta blockers, anticonvulsants, or Botox injections can be used to prevent migraines in some people.
  • Alternative therapies: Biofeedback, acupuncture, and meditation have all been shown to be effective for some people in preventing migraines.

Complications of Migraine

Migraines are associated with a small increased risk of ischaemic strokes, and a very small increased risk of mental health problems.

More About Migraine

The word migraine was derived from the Latin word “hemicranias” meaning “half” (hemi) “skull” (crania). Migraine is a common neurological disorder characterized by recurrent headaches. A migraine is usually a moderate or severe headache felt as a throbbing pain or a pulsing sensation, usually on one side of the head that gets worse when you move. Globally, approximately 15% of people are affected by migraine. In the Global Burden of Disease Study of 2010, it was ranked as the third most prevalent disorder in the world. It most often starts at puberty and is worst during middle age. it is more common in women than men. As of 2016, it is one of the most common causes of disability.
